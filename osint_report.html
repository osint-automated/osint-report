Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) Report

Date of Access: 11 March 2026

Date of Information: 11 March 2026

Reporting Period: 10 March - 11 March 2026

Distribution: Approved for public release; distribution is unlimited.

Combatant Commands: SOUTHCOM, EUCOM, PACOM, CENTCOM, AFRICOM

Intent: The intent of this report is to provide a high-level overview of significant military and geopolitical developments during the reporting period. The report consists of open-source reporting and updates on evolving conflicts, diplomatic initiatives, and strategic military actions involving countries of interest in the following Combatant Commands: SOUTHCOM, EUCOM, PACOM, CENTCOM, AFRICOM.

Executive Summary: During the reporting period, U.S. Southern Command conducted lethal counternarcotics strikes against suspected trafficking vessels in the Eastern Pacific and Caribbean and continued advisory coordination with Ecuador against cartel-linked groups while expanding hemispheric security cooperation. Ukrainian forces executed localized counterattacks in western Zaporizhzhia that forced Russian units into defensive positions and conducted drone strikes against Russian air defense infrastructure in Crimea, while Russia launched large-scale missile and drone attacks against Ukrainian infrastructure with limited territorial gains in Donetsk. China signaled interest in stabilizing relations with the United States, condemned U.S.-Israeli operations against Iran, and announced a 7 percent defense budget increase amid preparations for upcoming bilateral engagements. In the Middle East, U.S. and Israeli forces intensified strikes against Iranian military infrastructure and naval assets near the Strait of Hormuz, significantly degrading missile and drone capabilities as Iran launched retaliatory attacks against Israel and U.S. positions and installed Mojtaba Khamenei as supreme leader. In Africa, South Sudan prepared offensive operations near Akobo prompting civilian displacement, militants killed soldiers in northern Benin amid expanding Sahel insurgent activity, ceasefire violations persisted in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo, and U.S. Africa Command conducted an airstrike against ISIS-Somalia.

  • SOUTHCOM: During the reporting period, U.S. Southern Command conducted additional lethal kinetic strikes under Operation Southern Spear, targeting and destroying suspected narco-trafficking vessels in the Eastern Pacific and Caribbean on March 8-9, resulting in the deaths of multiple alleged narco-terrorists with no U.S. casualties reported, as part of ongoing efforts to disrupt cocaine flows along known smuggling routes. Joint advisory operations with Ecuadorian forces persisted against designated terrorist organizations including Los Lobos and Los Choneros, focusing on intelligence sharing, planning for raids, and dismantling drug networks without new reported kinetic engagements in the period. Trinidad and Tobago's Prime Minister Kamla Persad-Bissessar visited SOUTHCOM headquarters on March 9 to discuss bilateral defense cooperation and integration into the Americas Counter-Cartel Coalition framework established at the March 7 Shield of the Americas summit. No major escalations, new territorial incidents, or advancements materialized in the Guyana-Venezuela Essequibo dispute, which remained static pending ICJ proceedings amid post-Maduro stabilization dynamics in Venezuela; no significant cartel retaliatory violence, border clashes in Central America, or other kinetic developments surfaced in Mexico, Colombia, or additional South American theaters beyond routine counternarcotics coordination and follow-up to the coalition's formalization, despite broader regional attention on U.S. hemispheric security priorities amid concurrent Middle East commitments.

  • EUCOM: During the reporting period, Ukrainian forces sustained successful counterattacks in western Zaporizhzhia Oblast and southern sectors, pushing Russian units from positions near Prymorske, Novoyakolivka, northern Lukyanivske, and Pavlivka south of Zaporizhzhia City while halting advances near Orikhiv and in the Richne-Prymorske direction, forcing elements of the Russian 58th Combined Arms Army to adopt defensive postures and potentially disrupting linkages between the Hulyaipole and Orikhiv axes. Ukrainian drone operators targeted and damaged Russian military infrastructure, including a 64N6 Nadgorbok radar station in occupied Crimea that supported S-300PM and S-400 systems. Russia launched combined missile and drone barrages, deploying Iskander-M ballistic missiles and over 100 drones (including Shahed variants) that struck energy and civilian infrastructure in regions such as Kharkiv and Chernihiv, with Ukrainian defenses intercepting the majority but permitting some impacts that inflicted casualties and damage. Russian forces claimed marginal territorial gains in eastern Donetsk Oblast near Ryznykivka and other gray-zone areas amid continued pressure in directions like Pokrovsk and Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka, though overall frontline progress remained limited. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy visited eastern frontline positions to evaluate troop morale and operations while confirming the postponement of trilateral U.S.-Russia-Ukraine peace talks due to diplomatic priorities linked to concurrent U.S.-Israeli operations against Iran; no significant territorial shifts materialized in northern Kharkiv or Sumy Oblast, where Russian efforts stalled without advances.

  • PACOM: During the reporting period, China expressed hope that 2026 would become a landmark year for bilateral relations with the United States, as Foreign Minister Wang Yi emphasized during a press briefing on the sidelines of the National People's Congress Two Sessions, highlighting high-level leader exchanges as a strategic guarantee for managing differences through mutual respect, peaceful coexistence, and win-win cooperation. China condemned ongoing U.S.-Israeli military operations against Iran as unacceptable violations of sovereignty, regime-change attempts, and infringements on international law, with officials urging an immediate halt to strikes, protection of shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, prevention of escalation, and resumption of dialogue via diplomatic channels with regional and global parties. Beijing reaffirmed commitment to multi-level engagement with the United States, including preparations for mid-March trade negotiations in Paris and the anticipated Trump-Xi summit in Beijing from March 31 to April 2, while pledging protective measures for Chinese nationals and energy security amid conflict spillover risks to oil and LNG supplies critical to Chinese imports. China announced a 7 percent increase in its 2026 defense budget during the Two Sessions to advance military modernization and combat capabilities amid strategic rivalry. No direct U.S.-China military incidents, confrontations, exercises, or developments occurred in the Indo-Pacific, Taiwan Strait, or South China Sea domains.

  • CENTCOM: During the reporting period, U.S. and Israeli forces executed their most intense airstrikes of the conflict to date on March 10, deploying the highest number of fighters and bombers to target Iranian regime infrastructure in Tehran, Esfahan, Shiraz, and other provinces, including IRGC Quds Force sites, missile production and storage facilities, air defense systems such as radars and Hawk batteries at the Shahid Sattari site near Tehran, and additional military assets, with over 170 munitions dropped in one confirmed wave on March 9 extending into subsequent operations. The U.S. Central Command destroyed 16 Iranian minelayer vessels near the Strait of Hormuz on March 10 to counter threats to maritime traffic, while strikes continued to degrade Iran's ballistic missile and drone capabilities, reducing retaliatory barrages by approximately 90 percent overall. Israel launched renewed strikes on Hezbollah targets in Beirut's southern suburbs and southern Lebanon, contributing to cumulative Lebanese casualties exceeding 570 since related escalations intensified. Iran conducted multiple retaliatory missile and drone barrages—launching at least seven waves toward Israel between March 9 and 10, with one ballistic missile impacting an open area in Beit Shemesh without casualties, and additional salvos targeting U.S. military positions and allied sites in Gulf states including Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Iraq, though intercepts mitigated many effects amid Iran's shift toward "continuous strikes" rather than reciprocal responses. Iran named Mojtaba Khamenei as supreme leader following the death of Ali Khamenei, with hardline statements vowing prolonged resistance and rejecting U.S. demands for unconditional surrender; proxy forces including Hezbollah claimed 29 attacks on Israeli positions. U.S. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth emphasized maximum operational options and no shortage of military resources, while President Trump issued mixed signals on timelines, describing the campaign as far ahead of schedule yet threatening overwhelming escalation if Iran disrupted Strait of Hormuz shipping; cumulative Iranian deaths surpassed 1,300, Israeli civilian fatalities remained limited, and seven to eight U.S. service members died with about 140 wounded, as the conflict entered its 12th day with no ceasefire indications and oil prices reflecting ongoing energy route disruptions.

  • AFRICOM: During the reporting period, South Sudan People's Defense Forces enforced an evacuation order issued on March 6 for civilians, UNMISS, NGOs including MSF, and humanitarian facilities in opposition-held Akobo town and surrounding areas in Jonglei state, prompting mass displacement of thousands amid preparations for a government-led offensive and reports of looting at aid sites, as hostilities escalated between army and opposition forces with UN Human Rights Chief Volker Türk condemning brutal civilian killings, injuries, and destruction of water sources. The African Union Commission Chairperson condemned a jihadist attack in northern Benin near the Niger border that killed 15 soldiers, calling for accelerated ECOWAS regional brigade deployment under the African Standby Force to counter terrorism in West African border zones where JNIM and ISSP expanded operations in the Benin-Niger-Nigeria tri-border area. The United States, European Union, and International Contact Group for the Great Lakes expressed profound concern over continued ceasefire violations in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo involving M23 rebels, including drone usage and risks to civilians, urging recommitment to Doha and Washington agreements to end fighting. U.S. Africa Command conducted an airstrike in coordination with the Federal Government of Somalia targeting ISIS-Somalia on March 8, though no new al-Shabaab-specific strikes occurred in the period; no major kinetic developments materialized in Sudan beyond prior escalations in Kordofan, Libya, Ethiopia's Tigray region, or other theaters, while indirect concerns persisted over spillover from the U.S.-Israeli conflict with Iran potentially affecting Red Sea security, Horn stability, energy prices, and counterterrorism resource allocation in the Sahel.
