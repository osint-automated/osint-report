Intent: The intent of this report is to provide a high-level overview of significant military and geopolitical developments during the reporting period. The report consists of open-source reporting and updates on evolving conflicts, diplomatic initiatives, and strategic military actions involving countries of interest in the following Combatant Commands: SOUTHCOM, EUCOM, PACOM, CENTCOM, AFRICOM.

Executive Summary: During the reporting period, significant military and geopolitical updates were recorded across multiple regions. In Mexico, the assassination of cartel leader Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes led to violent reprisals and raised concerns about U.S. arms trafficking to drug cartels. In the Middle East, U.S. military operations against Iran escalated, with potential implications for Ukraine and regional security, as Israel increased troop deployments amid tensions with Hezbollah. U.S. officials warned of critical shortages in air defense munitions while the Iranian blockade of the Strait of Hormuz began disrupting global oil trade. Concurrently, civil unrest in South Sudan resulted in numerous fatalities, and the U.S. imposed sanctions on Rwanda for violations related to the unrest in the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo.

  • SOUTHCOM: During the reporting period, open source reporting indicated significant developments in military and geopolitical realms. In Mexico, the killing of prominent cartel leader Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes, known as "El Mencho," triggered violent retaliation, resulting in over 70 deaths. His burial in a golden casket in Guadalajara featured a heavy military presence, reflecting the cartel's entrenched power and influence. Additionally, Mexico’s defense minister disclosed that 80% of weapons seized from drug cartels originated from the United States, raising concerns about cross-border arms trafficking. Amid these tensions, preparations for the 2026 FIFA World Cup, co-hosted by Mexico, the United States, and Canada, were overshadowed by security and political issues, highlighting the complex challenges facing the region as it approaches the international event.

  • EUCOM: During the reporting period, Ukrainian forces conduct localized counteroffensive operations primarily in southern sectors (Zaporizhzhia and Donetsk regions), liberating approximately 300-460 square kilometers of territory and recapturing multiple eastern settlements for the first time achieving net territorial gains since 2023, while disrupting Russian advances through innovative use of jam-resistant fiber-optic drones and other systems. Russian forces maintain grinding offensives in eastern directions including Slovyansk, Pokrovsk, and Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka tactical areas, registering minor advances such as southeast of Bilytske and east of Kostyantynivka, but record their slowest overall progress since 2024 amid reported Starlink disruptions affecting command and control. Russia launches drone and missile strikes targeting Ukrainian military-industrial and energy infrastructure across multiple regions, with Ukrainian defenses downing significant numbers including eight Shahed drones over Kharkiv Oblast and intercepting others, while inflicting damage on Russian assets through strikes on S-300 radars, Sopka-2 and Kasta-2E2 systems, repair units, UAV command posts, and a new air-launched cruise missile sighting. Both sides exchange over 1,000 soldiers' bodies in a repatriation agreement as diplomatic efforts continue with Ukrainian negotiators engaging US peace envoys in Geneva amid Zelenskyy's assessment that Russian military objectives remain unfulfilled and face increasing difficulties in sustaining planned advances. No major shifts occur in northern Sumy Oblast operations, where Russian efforts fail to produce advances, as frontline dynamics reflect porous control lines and high attrition rates on both sides.

  • PACOM: During the reporting period, Asian stock markets experienced steep declines as investors reacted to the potential for a wider conflict. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi communicated to China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi, emphasizing Iran's commitment to protecting Chinese citizens amid escalating attacks from the US and Israel. In military advancements, US intelligence revealed that China has been rapidly constructing new nuclear submarines capable of targeting the US from its coastal waters, aiming for substantial fleet growth by 2035. Analysts expressed concerns that the US could soon face shortages of vital interceptor missiles necessary for its operations against Iran, causing unease among US allies in Asia like Japan and South Korea regarding their security against China and North Korea. China took countermeasures by placing Japanese military firms on its export control list, illustrating the tensions related to Japan's remilitarization. As hostilities continued, Iran's blockage of the Strait of Hormuz began to disrupt global oil trade significantly, prompting fears of rising prices and economic consequences across various regions.

  • CENTCOM: During the reporting period, U.S. and Israeli forces sustain intensive airstrikes on Iranian targets, including Tehran command centers, Basij militia hubs, internal security infrastructure, remaining ballistic missile sites, air defense radars, and naval facilities such as Konarak base, while dropping over 2,000 bombs cumulatively and maintaining 24/7 operations from seabed to space domains to achieve persistent air superiority. Israel conducts additional strikes on Hezbollah infrastructure in Beirut's southern suburbs and southern Lebanon in response to rocket and drone launches toward Haifa and northern areas. Iran executes multiple retaliatory waves—launching hundreds of ballistic missiles and thousands of drones across at least nine countries—targeting Israel (with intercepts over Tel Aviv, Jerusalem, and other sites causing injuries and minor structural damage), U.S. military positions in Bahrain (Fifth Fleet headquarters impacted), Kuwait (air bases and support facilities hit, including four U.S. Army reservists killed), Qatar (Al Udeid base and radar systems damaged), UAE (Dubai/Abu Dhabi airports and commercial zones struck, with fires and flight disruptions), Saudi Arabia (embassy and oil-related sites affected), and others including Jordan, Oman, and attempted reaches toward Iraq and Cyprus. Gulf states activate Patriot, THAAD, and other interceptors to mitigate impacts, though civilian infrastructure sustains hits near airports, ports, hotels, and residential areas, disrupting oil shipments, international travel, and regional stability. Hezbollah contributes rocket barrages from Lebanon, prompting Israeli ground pushes and airstrikes that displace populations and inflict casualties. No confirmed new major nuclear site strikes beyond prior assessments occur, as operations focus on degrading command structures, missile stocks, and regime control mechanisms amid Iran's fragmented leadership following Supreme Leader Khamenei's death.

  • AFRICOM: During the reporting period, open source reporting indicated that the West Indies and Zimbabwe cricket teams remained stranded in India due to disruptions in Middle East travel linked to ongoing regional conflicts. In South Sudan, at least 169 fatalities resulted from a recent attack involving both civilians and officials. The conflict in the Middle East also raised geopolitical concerns, with regional entities urging restraint while suspensions of flights continued. In South Africa, the political landscape shifted as the Umkhonto weSizwe movement sought collaboration with the African National Congress to establish a coalition government in a provincial assembly. Additionally, the U.S. imposed sanctions on the Rwandan military, citing violations of a peace agreement amid escalating tensions related to the situation in the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo.
