Intent: The intent of this report is to provide a high-level overview of significant military and geopolitical developments during the reporting period. The report consists of open-source reporting and updates on evolving conflicts, diplomatic initiatives, and strategic military actions involving countries of interest in the following Combatant Commands: SOUTHCOM, EUCOM, PACOM, CENTCOM, AFRICOM.

Executive Summary: During the reporting period, key military and geopolitical developments occurred globally. In South America, tensions escalated between Chile and the U.S. over a proposed Chinese submarine fiber optic cable project, while the Philippine Navy monitored Chinese naval activities in the West Philippine Sea. In the Middle East, the U.S. Navy received orders to escort vessels through the Strait of Hormuz amid rising regional tensions, and Israel conducted extensive strikes on Iranian nuclear and security targets, resulting in significant Iranian military losses. Concurrently, the U.S. sanctioned the Rwandan military for support of armed groups in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, while Ghana and Sierra Leone evacuated personnel from Iran due to conflicts escalating in the region. These developments highlighted the complexity of international relations and emerging conflicts amid ongoing military actions and strategic realignments.

  • SOUTHCOM: During the reporting period, open source reporting indicated that on 22 February, Mexican special forces killed Ruben Nemesio “El Mencho” Oseguera Cervantes, the leader of the Jalisco New Generation Cartel, in Tapalpa, Jalisco, after receiving intelligence from the U.S. military. Following his death, a significant deployment of the Mexican military and National Guard secured the funeral in Jalisco to maintain order. Geopolitical discussions also arose concerning issues related to Mexico's drug cartels, with mathematician Rafael Prieto-Curiel suggesting that analytical models could improve public safety. Rising tensions related to migration policies further complicated this dynamic, with officials asserting that both Mexico and China had "weaponized migration" to influence U.S. politics. Amidst these developments, preparations for the 2026 FIFA World Cup faced scrutiny, with the Estadio Azteca in Mexico still undergoing renovations just 100 days before the event.

  • EUCOM: During the reporting period, Ukrainian forces conducted localized counterattacks primarily in southern sectors including Zaporizhzhia and Donetsk regions, where they advanced in the Hulyaipole direction and maintained or improved positions near Novopavlivka and Oleksandrivka, contributing to net territorial gains that disrupted Russian momentum. Russian forces registered marginal advances in eastern directions such as the Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka tactical area (east and southeast of Sofiivka) and near Slovyansk (central Drobysheve and southeast of Stavky), but achieved their slowest overall frontline progress since 2024 amid reported disruptions to command systems. Russia launched drone and missile strikes against Ukrainian railway infrastructure in Mykolaiv Oblast and attempted attacks on moving trains, damaging assets and intensifying efforts to degrade logistics, while Ukrainian defenses intercepted multiple threats including Shahed drones over Kharkiv Oblast. Both sides completed a prisoner exchange of 200 individuals each with plans for an additional 300, as diplomatic channels explored postponement of upcoming trilateral U.S.-Russia-Ukraine peace talks due to the concurrent U.S.-Israeli operations against Iran. No significant shifts materialized in northern Sumy Oblast, where Russian operations stalled without territorial changes, as high attrition persisted along porous frontline segments.

  • PACOM: During the reporting period, China condemned U.S.-Israeli military strikes on Iran as unacceptable violations of sovereignty and regime-change efforts, with Foreign Minister Wang Yi urging an immediate cessation of operations, prevention of escalation, and resumption of dialogue through diplomatic communications with multiple parties including Russia, Iran, Israel, and regional states. China expressed deep concern over potential spillover effects disrupting energy security and the Strait of Hormuz, while pledging to protect its citizens in Iran and affirming its opposition to infringement on other nations' territorial integrity without direct military involvement. Beijing signaled continued commitment to multi-level communication with the United States, reaffirmed defense of its core interests and red lines on sovereignty, security, and development, and highlighted preparations for mid-March trade discussions in Paris between USTR Scott Bessent and Vice Premier He Lifeng ahead of the anticipated Trump-Xi summit in Beijing at the end of March. China announced a 7% increase in its 2026 defense budget to support modernization efforts amid strategic competition with the U.S., while setting a lower GDP growth target of 4.5%-5% reflecting geopolitical uncertainties including ongoing trade tensions and Middle East instability. No direct U.S.-China military incidents, confrontations, or exercises occurred in the Indo-Pacific, Taiwan Strait, or South China Sea domains.

  • CENTCOM: During the reporting period, U.S. and Israeli forces maintained sustained airstrikes on Iranian military infrastructure, targeting Revolutionary Guard command centers in Tehran, Basij militia sites, missile launchers, air defense systems, and naval facilities, with Israel conducting over 60 attack waves and the U.S. sinking an Iranian frigate near Sri Lanka via submarine torpedo while accelerating operations across the region. Israel expanded strikes against Hezbollah in southern Lebanon and Beirut suburbs in response to ongoing rocket and drone launches from Lebanese territory toward northern Israel. Iran executed multiple retaliatory barrages, launching ballistic missiles and drones that struck or targeted U.S. military sites including Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar, facilities in Bahrain (Fifth Fleet headquarters area with reported explosions), Kuwait, UAE (consulate in Dubai hit by drone causing fire), Saudi Arabia (embassy strikes), and others across Gulf states, while additional missiles impacted or were intercepted near Jordan and attempted reaches toward Incirlik in Turkey. Defensive systems in Gulf countries and Israel intercepted many projectiles, but confirmed impacts disrupted infrastructure near airports, ports, energy facilities, and residential areas, with QatarEnergy declaring force majeure on LNG production amid Strait of Hormuz disruptions. Iranian proxy forces including Iraqi militias continued attacks on U.S. positions, Hezbollah sustained operations against Israel, and no major new nuclear site damage beyond prior limited effects occurred, as operations focused on degrading Iranian missile stocks, command structures, and naval capabilities amid escalating regional involvement and rising casualties exceeding 1,000 in Iran from cumulative strikes.

    • Analyst Comment: The US launched Operation Epic Fury on 28 February 2026. Officials justify the campaign as preemptive action to eliminate imminent threats to US forces and allies, destroy Iran's reconstituted nuclear weapon potential, and neutralize ballistic missile capabilities threatening regional and homeland security. Trump frames the strikes as a response to Iran's decades-long support for terrorism and aggression, with rationales shifting among preemption, nonproliferation, and regime degradation.
    • Operation Epic Fury has crippled Iran's crude export capacity, halting 80-90% of shipments to China via shadow fleets and effectively blocking the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint for ~30% of global seaborne crude. Combined with prior US actions in Venezuela, this forces Chinese refiners to draw on 4-6 months of strategic reserves and pivot to higher-cost alternatives, including Russian crude. Disruptions drive global oil prices up 10-13%, with further spikes above $100/barrel likely amid elevated insurance premiums and rerouting. Although US statements avoid explicitly targeting China, the strikes indirectly pressure Beijing by constraining discounted energy access that underpins manufacturing and regional influence, while prompting China to urge de-escalation and recalibrate amid broader Asian economic strain.

  • AFRICOM: During the reporting period, open source reporting indicated significant military and geopolitical developments. On 2 March, the United States imposed sanctions on the Rwandan military and four senior commanders due to their support for the M23 armed group in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Concurrently, Ghana and Sierra Leone evacuated their embassy staff and citizens from Iran amid escalating tensions in the Middle East. Nigeria's military ramped up patrols to safeguard farmers as the 2026 planting season began, aiming to prevent a repeat of the deadly attacks seen in 2025. In UK policy changes, the government stopped issuing study visas for nationals from Myanmar, Afghanistan, Cameroon, and Sudan, citing an increase in asylum claims from these countries. Meanwhile, concerns grew around the status of Morocco hosting the Women's Africa Cup of Nations, with imminent decisions expected from the Confederation of African Football less than two weeks before the tournament. Additionally, four children reportedly died in Djibouti's Dhikil region following an explosion alleged to be caused by a drone strike.
