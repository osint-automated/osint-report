Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) Report

Date of Access: 28 March 2026

Date of Information: 28 March 2026

Reporting Period: 27 March - 28 March 2026

Distribution: Approved for public release; distribution is unlimited.

Combatant Commands: SOUTHCOM, EUCOM, PACOM, CENTCOM, AFRICOM

Intent: The intent of this report is to provide a high-level overview of significant military and geopolitical developments during the reporting period. The report consists of open-source reporting and updates on evolving conflicts, diplomatic initiatives, and strategic military actions involving countries of interest in the following Combatant Commands: SOUTHCOM, EUCOM, PACOM, CENTCOM, AFRICOM.

Executive Summary: During the reporting period, Russian forces intensified their aerial offensive against Ukraine, deploying nearly 1,000 drones and missiles that led to significant civilian casualties and damage to critical infrastructure on 27 March, while Ukrainian forces conducted counter-strikes aimed at diminishing Russian export capacities. The People's Liberation Army increased its aerial maneuvers around Taiwan, crossing the median line on multiple occasions while retrofitting outdated aircraft for potential future conflicts, as Taiwan's military stayed vigilant. Israeli airstrikes targeted Iranian nuclear facilities, prompting retaliatory missile attacks on Israel and U.S. bases in Saudi Arabia, amidst ongoing diplomatic tensions over Iran's nuclear program. Concurrently, clashes between Congolese forces and AFC/M23 rebels escalated, complicating regional stability and drawing international scrutiny toward Rwanda. In Somalia, the movement of heavy weaponry by the National Army highlighted shifting security dynamics as regional tensions grew, while militant assaults in the Sahel underscored persistent challenges to state control across Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger.

  • SOUTHCOM: During the reporting period, Central and South American states registered no major geopolitical escalations or military engagements, as U.S.-led counter-cartel operations in Ecuador maintained steady tempo without new strikes after 8 March, Mexican security forces neutralized residual CJNG elements in Jalisco amid nationwide roadblocks on 27 March, and Venezuelan authorities approved mining law amendments on 27 March that eased U.S. transactions with state gold firm Minerven under General License 51, while Brazilian, Colombian, and Peruvian militaries conducted routine border patrols absent interstate tensions.

  • EUCOM: During the reporting period, Russian forces launched nearly 1,000 drones and missiles against Ukrainian cities from Lviv to Kharkiv on 27 March, inflicting civilian casualties, damaging energy infrastructure, transport networks, and a UNESCO site in Lviv while escalating battalion assaults near Sloviansk, Pokrovsk, and Huliaypole. Ukrainian General Staff recorded 150 frontline clashes across multiple sectors, with Russian units executing 68 airstrikes, 227 guided bomb strikes, 9,190 kamikaze drone attacks, and 4,000 artillery shells; Ukrainian forces countered by striking five Russian troop concentrations and Russian oil ports and refineries, reducing Moscow's export capacity by approximately 40 percent. In the Kupiansk direction, Russian assault troops advanced to the center of Kostiantynivka. Geopolitically, Ukraine finalised preliminary deals with Middle Eastern partners on 27 March to procure systems countering Iranian-supplied drones, while US Secretary of State Rubio categorically denied on 28 March claims that Washington conditioned security guarantees on Kyiv ceding Donbas territories, as peace talks stalled amid US pressure for concessions.

  • PACOM: During the reporting period, People's Liberation Army (PLA) aircraft executed six sorties around Taiwan, with four crossing the median line into Taiwan's southwestern and eastern air defense identification zone (ADIZ), while ten People's Liberation Army Navy (PLAN) vessels and two official ships operated in the vicinity, prompting Republic of China Armed Forces to monitor and respond accordingly as reported by Taiwan's Ministry of National Defense on 27 March. PLA stationed obsolete J-6 fighter aircraft, retrofitted as unmanned attack drones, at six forward air bases along the Taiwan Strait coast, enabling potential saturation strikes to overwhelm defenses in initial conflict phases, according to open-source intelligence and satellite imagery analysis released by Japan's National Institute for Defense Studies on 27 March. No significant PLA activities occurred in the South China Sea or East China Sea, and US Indo-Pacific Command issued no statements on Chinese operations.

  • CENTCOM: During the reporting period, Israeli forces executed precision airstrikes on multiple Iranian nuclear and industrial facilities, including the Shahid Khondab Heavy Water Complex at Arak, the Ardakan yellowcake production plant in Yazd Province, a uranium processing plant, a nuclear research facility, steel mills in Isfahan and Ahvaz, and ballistic missile production sites in western Iran and central Tehran, resulting in one fatality and over thirty injuries at the steel complexes with no reported radiological contamination. Iran retaliated with missile and drone barrages targeting Israel's urban centers, including Tel Aviv and Jerusalem on 27 March, killing one civilian and prompting emergency responses to eleven impact sites, while simultaneously striking the US-occupied Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia, injuring twelve American troops—two seriously—and destroying or damaging three KC-135 aerial refueling aircraft. Yemen's Houthi proxies, backed by Tehran, launched their first direct missile toward Israel early on 28 March, intercepted near Beersheba and Israel's primary nuclear research center. US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff expressed optimism for direct negotiations with Iranian counterparts that week, citing a pending fifteen-point ceasefire proposal demanding nuclear restrictions and reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, as President Trump extended a deadline for strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure by ten days amid claims of productive diplomacy during a Saudi investment conference. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi rejected the US terms, issued a counter five-point plan asserting sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz and demanding reparations, and vowed a heavy retaliatory price for the attacks, which he accused of US-Israeli coordination in violation of the diplomatic pause. Israeli officials warned of campaign escalation and expansion, while the International Atomic Energy Agency urged restraint following the nuclear site strikes.

  • AFRICOM: During the reporting period, MONUSCO warned that Democratic Republic of Congo government forces clashed with AFC/M23 rebels, expanding hostilities into Tshopo Province through offensive drone strikes and systematic GPS jamming that endangered civilians at Bangoka Airport in Kisangani and sites in Goma on 27 March. Congolese authorities accused Rwanda-supported M23 elements of sovereignty violations, while AFC/M23 withdrew from Walikale and Lubero districts, enabling Wazalendo militia advances amid intensifying international pressure on Rwanda. Somali National Army convoys transported heavy weaponry toward Baidoa on 27 March to replace Ethiopian security contingents, traversing Al-Shabaab-held areas like Leego en route to Burhakaba, as Southwest State forces mobilised defensively against Federal Government troops and reports emerged of Egyptian-supplied arms influxes destabilising the region. JNIM and Islamic State Sahel Province militants executed lethal assaults across Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger, contesting state control in northern Mali's Inkadewane sector where Malian forces countered insurgent blockades. Nigerian air forces struck Islamic State West Africa Province positions in Sambisa Forest amid persistent communal violence.
